1. University Basics

Understand the foundational concepts of higher education before building your application strategy.

College vs. University

Colleges generally offer undergraduate degrees (Bachelor's), while Universities are larger institutions offering both undergraduate and graduate (Master's/PhD) programs with a heavy focus on research.

Public vs. Private

Public universities are funded by state governments, making them cheaper for in-state residents. Private universities rely on tuition and endowments, offering smaller class sizes but higher sticker prices.

2. Tuition & Cost of Attendance

"Cost of Attendance" (COA) is the total estimated cost for one year of college. It includes more than just your classes.

Direct Costs (Billed by the University)
Tuition: The cost of your academic classes.

Room & Board: The cost of your dormitory room and campus meal plan.
Indirect Costs (Hidden Expenses)
Books & Supplies: Textbooks and laptops (usually estimated at $1,200/year).

Personal Expenses: Laundry, transportation, winter clothing, and flights home.

3. The Application Package

Admissions officers use a holistic review process. They don't just look at grades; they look at the whole package.

Transcripts & GPA

Your academic record from Grade 9-12. Universities check if you took challenging classes (AP, IB, Honors) rather than just taking easy classes for a perfect GPA.

Recommendations

Letters from two teachers and one counselor. You should ask teachers who know your work ethic and character, not just the ones who gave you an A+.

4. Essays & Personal Statements

The personal statement is your chance to show the admissions committee your personality, values, and voice.

Top 3 Essay Mistakes to Avoid
1. The Resume Rehash: Do not just list your awards. They already have your resume. Tell a story about a specific moment.

2. Using Big Words Falsely: Do not use a thesaurus to sound "smart." Write in your authentic, 17-year-old voice.

3. The "Savior" Trip: Avoid writing cliché essays about how a 2-week volunteer trip completely changed your perspective on life. Focus on long-term, local impact.

5. High School Roadmap Timeline

Grade 9 & 10 (Exploration)

Focus on exploring interests. Join clubs, find what you are passionate about, and maintain strong foundational grades.

Grade 11 (Preparation)

The most important academic year. Take leadership roles in your clubs, take SAT/ACT tests if required, and start building a balanced list of Reach, Target, and Safety schools.

Grade 12 - Autumn (Execution)

Write personal statements, request recommendation letters early, and submit applications via Early Action or Early Decision.

6. Terminology Dictionary

FAFSA

Free Application for Federal Student Aid. The official form US citizens fill out to determine financial aid eligibility.

Rolling Admission

A policy where a university reviews applications as they are received, rather than waiting for a hard deadline. Apply early!

Need-Blind

An admission policy where the university does not consider your financial situation when deciding whether to accept you.

Waitlist

You were not accepted or rejected. If accepted students choose not to attend, the university may offer their spot to you later in the summer.
